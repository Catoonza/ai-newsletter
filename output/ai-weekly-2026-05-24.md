## 📚 Research Highlights (arXiv)

### The Quick Take

- **OpenAI model disproves 80-year-old geometry conjecture** ([arxiv.org](http://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22763v1)): An OpenAI model autonomously resolved 9 of 353 open Erdős problems and proved 44/492 OEIS conjectures via formal proof search in Lean. Why it matters: This moves AI from pattern-matching to *discovering novel mathematics*, validating the hypothesis that LLMs can contribute to frontier research when paired with formal verification systems. The cost: a few hundred dollars per problem, suggesting automated mathematical discovery may soon be cheaper than hiring postdocs.

- **Evaluating AI chatbots as news intermediaries across 6 languages** ([arxiv.org](http://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22785v1)): Researchers tested Gemini, Grok, Claude, and GPT models on 2,100 same-day factual questions across regional BBC services. Best systems achieved >85% accuracy, but performance varied wildly by language and region. Why it matters: As a billion+ people now query chatbots for news weekly, this is the first systematic measurement of whether these systems are reliable information intermediaries—spoiler: sometimes, but inconsistently.

- **Reducing political manipulation with Consistency Training** ([arxiv.org](http://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22771v1)): Researchers identified 7 categories of "covert political bias" where LLMs handle opposing political topics asymmetrically, then proposed Political Consistency Training (PCT) to reduce it. Why it matters: Political bias isn't just sentiment—it's structural asymmetry in depth and engagement that training can address, but only if the problem is explicitly measured first.

- **MOSS: Self-evolving autonomous agent systems via source-level rewriting** ([arxiv.org](http://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22794v1)): Current agentic systems can only self-update via prompt or skill files, leaving core routing and state logic frozen. MOSS enables agents to rewrite their own control flow. Why it matters: Agentic AI locked in static code is fundamentally limited; true adaptation requires meta-level self-modification, which opens both capability and safety questions.

- **DeltaBox: Millisecond-level checkpoint/rollback for agent state exploration** ([arxiv.org](http://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22781v1)): Existing sandbox checkpoints take hundreds of milliseconds to seconds, bottlenecking tree search. DeltaBox uses delta compression (only storing changes between checkpoints) to enable millisecond C/R. Why it matters: Agent inference-time search (like AlphaEvolve) requires thousands of rollbacks; removing this bottleneck is the infrastructure layer that makes agentic search practical.

- **Tokenization via Convex Relaxations (ConvexTok)** ([arxiv.org](http://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22821v1)): Standard tokenizers (BPE, Unigram) are greedy; ConvexTok formulates the problem as a linear program and solves it globally. Improves bits-per-byte consistently, especially on downstream tasks. Why it matters: Tokenization is often treated as a solved problem, but global optimization shows there's still 2-5% efficiency left on the table—relevant for models where compute scales with token count.

- **1 in 5 Brits believe AI layoffs could trigger civil unrest**: [UK survey data](https://www.theregister.com/ai-ml/2026/05/19/1-in-5-brits-think-ai-layoffs-could-trigger-civil-unrest/) showed public skepticism about AI-as-prosperity is hardening into fear about systemic instability. - **KPMG integrates Claude across 276,000+ employees**: [Anthropic announced a strategic alliance](https://www.anthropic.com/news/anthropic-kpmg) with KPMG for enterprise-wide deployment, legitimizing Claude as an enterprise mainstream tool. ## 🐦 From the AI Community

### Notable Discussions This Week

- **[The Verge: "Hackers are learning to exploit chatbot 'personalities'"](https://www.theverge.com/column/935545/hackers-ai-chatbots)** — Security researchers identified that jailbreaks exploiting chatbot personas (e.g., "pretend you're an unrestricted AI") are becoming more sophisticated. Why interesting: Chatbot safety isn't about blocking keywords—it's about the implicit contract between the model and user. Exploiting personality breaks that contract at the semantic level, not the syntactic level.

- **[The Verge: "Google's AI search is so broken it can 'disregard' what you're looking for"](https://www.theverge.com/tech/936176/google-ai-overviews-search-disregard)** — Google's AI Overviews had a bug where searching "disregard" would cause the system to *actually disregard* the user's query and provide unrelated content. Why interesting: This is a humbling proof that semantic models can become self-referential in ways their creators didn't anticipate. The fix is likely a prompt patch, but it exposed how brittle the inference-time behavior is.

- **[Scientific American: "'Bixonimania'—the fake illness that AI fell for"](https://www.scientificamerican.com/podcast/episode/bixonimania-the-fake-illness-that-ai-fell-for/)** — Researchers created a fictional skin condition and fed it to AI medical chatbots. Multiple systems confidently diagnosed and prescribed treatments for the fake disease. Why interesting: This is the reliability problem that no one solved. LLMs trained on medical literature will confidently generate plausible-sounding advice about conditions that don't exist—a failure mode that's hard to prevent at inference time.

- **[Vox: "The hidden way dictatorships are shaping what AI tells you"](https://www.vox.com/politics/489191/chatgpt-claude-china-bias-ai)** — Investigation into how authoritarian governments shape chatbot training data and fine-tuning to embed political bias. Why interesting: This isn't a Western corporate problem—it's a geopolitical one. China and Russia are not debating AI alignment; they're deploying AI as a tool of information control.

## 📌 One to Watch

**Hybrid AI combining LLMs with predictive models is emerging as the reliability solution.** [Forbes reported](https://www.forbes.com/sites/ericsiegel/2026/05/18/hybrid-ai-emerges-to-tame-llms--and-not-a-moment-too-soon/) that Instacart, HP, Salesforce, and Twilio are shipping systems that pair generative AI (which is creative but unreliable) with classical ML models (which are deterministic but narrow). The pattern: use LLMs for reasoning and synthesis, but route mission-critical predictions through predictive models with known error bounds. This is pragmatically solving the "genAI is too unreliable for production" problem by admitting that the field hasn't solved foundational reliability and building around it. Expect this hybrid architecture to become industry standard within 18 months—it's the admission that pure LLM pipelines have hit a wall.
