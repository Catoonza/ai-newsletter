Vol. 2026 · No. 21 What happened in AI this week — and why it matters Week of May 24, 2026
This week: The week crystallized a growing disconnect between AI industry leadership and public sentiment. While executives praised AI's transformative potential at commencement ceremonies, graduates booed them off stage—a visceral rejection that reflected deeper anxieties about job displacement and AI's governance. Simultaneously, the regulatory landscape fractured: Trump's AI executive order stalled amid internal concerns, OpenAI positioned itself for IPO with a "reverse federalism" strategy to avoid federal oversight, and the Vatican issued its first papal encyclical on AI ethics. The technical progress continued relentlessly—Google integrated AI into search at scale, multiple companies announced agentic breakthroughs, and researchers proved an 80-year-old geometry conjecture with AI—but the narrative shifted sharply toward questions of power, accountability, and who benefits.

This removes a major barrier for agentic AI in regulated industries—a pattern across AWS blog posts this week showing healthcare, finance, and radiology agents moving from proof-of-concept to production.

§ 02Research Highlights
 
The Quick Take
—OpenAI model disproves 80-year-old geometry conjecture (arxiv.org): An OpenAI model autonomously resolved 9 of 353 open Erdős problems and proved 44/492 OEIS conjectures via formal proof search in Lean. Why it matters: This moves AI from pattern-matching to discovering novel mathematics, validating the hypothesis that LLMs can contribute to frontier research when paired with formal verification systems. The cost: a few hundred dollars per problem, suggesting automated mathematical discovery may soon be cheaper than hiring postdocs.
—Evaluating AI chatbots as news intermediaries across 6 languages (arxiv.org): Researchers tested Gemini, Grok, Claude, and GPT models on 2,100 same-day factual questions across regional BBC services. Best systems achieved >85% accuracy, but performance varied wildly by language and region. Why it matters: As a billion+ people now query chatbots for news weekly, this is the first systematic measurement of whether these systems are reliable information intermediaries—spoiler: sometimes, but inconsistently.
—Reducing political manipulation with Consistency Training (arxiv.org): Researchers identified 7 categories of "covert political bias" where LLMs handle opposing political topics asymmetrically, then proposed Political Consistency Training (PCT) to reduce it. Why it matters: Political bias isn't just sentiment—it's structural asymmetry in depth and engagement that training can address, but only if the problem is explicitly measured first.
—MOSS: Self-evolving autonomous agent systems via source-level rewriting (arxiv.org): Current agentic systems can only self-update via prompt or skill files, leaving core routing and state logic frozen. MOSS enables agents to rewrite their own control flow. Why it matters: Agentic AI locked in static code is fundamentally limited; true adaptation requires meta-level self-modification, which opens both capability and safety questions.
—DeltaBox: Millisecond-level checkpoint/rollback for agent state exploration (arxiv.org): Existing sandbox checkpoints take hundreds of milliseconds to seconds, bottlenecking tree search. DeltaBox uses delta compression (only storing changes between checkpoints) to enable millisecond C/R. Why it matters: Agent inference-time search (like AlphaEvolve) requires thousands of rollbacks; removing this bottleneck is the infrastructure layer that makes agentic search practical.
—Tokenization via Convex Relaxations (ConvexTok) (arxiv.org): Standard tokenizers (BPE, Unigram) are greedy; ConvexTok formulates the problem as a linear program and solves it globally. Improves bits-per-byte consistently, especially on downstream tasks. Why it matters: Tokenization is often treated as a solved problem, but global optimization shows there's still 2-5% efficiency left on the table—relevant for models where compute scales with token count.
§ 03AI in Business & Industry
 
The Quick Take
—Graduates boo tech CEOs praising AI: Former Google CEO Eric Schmidt was booed at University of Arizona commencement. Schmidt wasn't alone—this became a pattern across 2026 graduation season. Why it matters: The executive class has fundamentally misread public mood. They're selling AI-as-progress; workers and students hear AI-as-displacement. This gap will shape policy and hiring for years.
—Andrej Karpathy joins Anthropic; Sam Altman's position weakens: Karpathy, a founding member of OpenAI, moved to Anthropic as Musk-Altman trial concluded. This signals both that Anthropic is consolidating top talent and that OpenAI's internal stability is uncertain. Why it matters: The AI Cold War between OpenAI and Anthropic just acquired a heavyweight defection that hints at which company insiders believe will win.
—Trump's AI executive order stalled after internal concerns: The draft order was pulled Thursday after Trump raised "many" concerns, reportedly because it proposed voluntary model-sharing with government rather than mandatory disclosure. Why it matters: The absence of a Trump AI order is itself a policy signal—it creates a regulatory vacuum that OpenAI is explicitly trying to fill via "reverse federalism" (promoting state-level rules instead of federal ones to fragment regulation).
—Steve Bannon petitions Trump for pre-release AI model review: Over 60 Trump allies signed a letter calling for government approval of new models before release. Why it matters: This is Trump's right flank demanding more AI oversight, contradicting the tech-friendly narrative. It's pressure from national security hawks, not progressives.
—1 in 5 Brits believe AI layoffs could trigger civil unrest: UK survey data showed public skepticism about AI-as-prosperity is hardening into fear about systemic instability. Why it matters: This is the public's glass-half-empty version of the CEO optimism on stage—same technology, opposite interpretation.
—KPMG integrates Claude across 276,000+ employees: Anthropic announced a strategic alliance with KPMG for enterprise-wide deployment, legitimizing Claude as an enterprise mainstream tool. Why it matters: This is scale validation—not a startup using one AI model, but a Big Four professional services firm betting its operations on Claude.
—OpenAI's IPO filing imminent: Reuters reported that OpenAI is ready to file within days now that the Musk trial concluded. Why it matters: Going public would make OpenAI's governance legible to shareholders and SEC oversight, forcing public disclosure of model safety practices, training costs, and board composition.
—Jeff Bezos tells Amazon workers to embrace AI displacement as a "gift": His message crystallized elite disconnection. Workers heard: your job is being automated and you should be grateful. Why it matters: This framing will drive recruitment friction and regulatory backlash—companies can't both automate at scale and expect goodwill.
§ 04AI Tools & Products
 
The Quick Take
—Cursor recognized as Gartner leader for enterprise AI coding agents (Cursor blog): Cursor released Composer 2.5 with substantially improved agentic behavior on long-horizon tasks. Why it matters: Cursor is becoming the canonical IDE for AI-assisted development—when integrated with Codex or Claude, it's reshaping how engineers ship code.

The implication: cheap inference (especially on chips like the Raspberry Pi + ESP32 combo detailed in XDA) is enabling edge AI that was impossible two years ago.
—Kioxia PCIe 5.0 XG10 SSD series (14,000 MB/s read speeds): The infrastructure layer is optimizing for AI workloads—SSDs are now specced for ML engineers running feature pipelines and training. Why it matters: Hardware is racing ahead of software bottlenecks, suggesting the field is approaching a compute saturation point where I/O becomes the constraint.
—Sony puts cameras in earbuds for AI vision: Hardware makers are embedding multimodal AI directly into wearables. Why it matters: AI isn't just software anymore—it's becoming a hardware material embedded in consumer devices, with privacy and consent implications that aren't yet legally settled.
—Spotify AI remix tool launches (Spotify blog via The Verge): Spotify partnered with UMG to let users generate AI remixes of licensed music. Why it matters: This is the first major music platform to commercialize AI generation within the licensing framework. It normalizes AI music at scale while avoiding the copyright lawsuits that plagued earlier tools.
—Samsung memory chip workers negotiated $340,000 annual bonuses: Tentative deal settled an 18-day strike threat. Why it matters: AI chip manufacturing is so capital-intensive and specialized that workers have leverage. This sets a floor for AI infrastructure worker compensation.
§ 05From the AI Community
 
Notable Discussions This Week
—The Verge: "Hackers are learning to exploit chatbot 'personalities'" — Security researchers identified that jailbreaks exploiting chatbot personas (e.g., "pretend you're an unrestricted AI") are becoming more sophisticated. Why interesting: Chatbot safety isn't about blocking keywords—it's about the implicit contract between the model and user. Exploiting personality breaks that contract at the semantic level, not the syntactic level.
—The Verge: "Google's AI search is so broken it can 'disregard' what you're looking for" — Google's AI Overviews had a bug where searching "disregard" would cause the system to actually disregard the user's query and provide unrelated content. Why interesting: This is a humbling proof that semantic models can become self-referential in ways their creators didn't anticipate. The fix is likely a prompt patch, but it exposed how brittle the inference-time behavior is.
—Scientific American: "'Bixonimania'—the fake illness that AI fell for" — Researchers created a fictional skin condition and fed it to AI medical chatbots. Multiple systems confidently diagnosed and prescribed treatments for the fake disease. Why interesting: This is the reliability problem that no one solved. LLMs trained on medical literature will confidently generate plausible-sounding advice about conditions that don't exist—a failure mode that's hard to prevent at inference time.
—Vox: "The hidden way dictatorships are shaping what AI tells you" — Investigation into how authoritarian governments shape chatbot training data and fine-tuning to embed political bias. Why interesting: This isn't a Western corporate problem—it's a geopolitical one. China and Russia are not debating AI alignment; they're deploying AI as a tool of information control.
§ 06One to Watch
 
📌  Editor's Pick

Hybrid AI combining LLMs with predictive models is emerging as the reliability solution. Forbes reported that Instacart, HP, Salesforce, and Twilio are shipping systems that pair generative AI (which is creative but unreliable) with classical ML models (which are deterministic but narrow). The pattern: use LLMs for reasoning and synthesis, but route mission-critical predictions through predictive models with known error bounds. This is pragmatically solving the "genAI is too unreliable for production" problem by admitting that the field hasn't solved foundational reliability and building around it. Expect this hybrid architecture to become industry standard within 18 months—it's the admission that pure LLM pipelines have hit a wall.
