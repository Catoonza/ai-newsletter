## This Week at a Glance

AI's collision with political power dominated this week's news cycle: Anthropic won a court injunction blocking the Pentagon from blacklisting it as a "supply chain risk," while the White House lost its top AI policy architect when David Sacks stepped down as AI and Crypto Czar. Meanwhile, the technology itself kept moving — Apple signalled a multi-chatbot future for Siri, Google launched a new voice AI model, and OpenAI quietly shelved its Sora video app to bet on robotics. ## 🚀 Model Releases & Provider Updates

### The Quick Take

- **Google DeepMind released [Gemini 3.1 Flash Live](https://deepmind.google/blog/gemini-3-1-flash-live-making-audio-ai-more-natural-and-reliable/)**, a voice-optimised model with lower latency and improved naturalness for real-time conversation.
- **Google also launched [Lyria 3 Pro](https://deepmind.google/blog/lyria-3-pro-create-longer-tracks-in-more/)**, its music generation model, now capable of producing longer, structurally coherent tracks and integrated into more Google surfaces.
- **Apple's iOS 27 will reportedly [let users plug any third-party chatbot into Siri](https://www.theverge.com/tech/902048/apple-siri-ai-chatbot-update-ios-27)** — including Claude and Gemini — and separately, The Information reports Apple can "distil" Google's full Gemini model for on-device use.
- **Amazon Bedrock expanded to [New Zealand](https://aws.amazon.com/blogs/machine-learning/run-generative-ai-inference-with-amazon-bedrock-in-asia-pacific-new-zealand/)**, offering Anthropic Claude and Amazon Nova models with regional inference, and launched a [bidirectional streaming API for Amazon Polly](https://aws.amazon.com/blogs/machine-learning/introducing-amazon-polly-bidirectional-streaming-real-time-speech-synthesis-for-conversational-ai/) for real-time text-to-speech in conversational apps.
- **OpenAI is [discontinuing the Sora video app](https://www.businessinsider.com/openai-discontinues-sora-video-app-amid-robotics-shift-compute-limitations-2026-3)** to redirect compute and focus toward robotics, while separately launching a [Safety Bug Bounty program](https://openai.com/index/safety-bug-bounty) targeting agentic vulnerabilities and prompt injection.
- **Cursor released [real-time reinforcement learning for its Composer feature](https://cursor.com/blog/real-time-rl-for-composer)** and added [self-hosted cloud agent support](https://cursor.com/blog/self-hosted-cloud-agents), giving enterprises control over where agent workloads run.
- **Google Gemini introduced [Import Memory and Import Chat History](https://www.theverge.com/ai-artificial-intelligence/902085/google-gemini-import-memory-chat-history)**, letting users port context from other AI assistants — a direct response to Anthropic's similar Claude feature.

### 🔬 Deeper Dive

The Apple story is deceptively significant. For years, Siri has been a walled garden — Apple's assistant, Apple's models, full stop. The reported iOS 27 change treats the underlying AI like a browser engine: Siri becomes a shell that routes queries to whichever model the user prefers, whether Claude, Gemini, or something else entirely. This is a platform shift, not just a feature. It echoes how the smartphone era separated the browser from the operating system, and it suggests Apple has concluded that being the *distribution layer* for AI may be more durable than competing on model quality alone. Separately, the report that Apple can "distil" Google's large Gemini model for on-device use hints at a deeper technical partnership — distillation being the process of training a smaller, faster model to mimic the behaviour of a much larger one, enabling frontier-level responses without cloud round-trips.

The Google Gemini memory import feature is a small but pointed move in what is becoming a genuine portability war. AI assistants accumulate value over time through personalisation — they learn your preferences, your writing style, your context. Locking that in has been an implicit retention strategy. By actively courting users to import memory *from* competitors, Google is betting its model quality is strong enough to win converts, and that openness is the better long-term play. Watch whether OpenAI follows.

## 📚 Research Highlights (arXiv)

### The Quick Take

- **[Self-Improvement of Large Language Models](http://arxiv.org/abs/2603.25681v1)**: A comprehensive technical survey argues that as LLMs approach human-level performance in some domains, human feedback is becoming an insufficient training signal — making automated self-improvement loops not just convenient but necessary.
- **[WriteBack-RAG](http://arxiv.org/abs/2603.25737v1)**: A new framework treats a RAG system's knowledge base as a *trainable* component, distilling relevant document fragments back into a compact, indexed corpus — potentially closing the gap between static retrieval and dynamic learning.
- **[RC2: Cycle-Consistent Reinforcement Learning](http://arxiv.org/abs/2603.25720v1)**: Proposes enforcing *cross-modal consistency* as a training signal — if a model gives contradictory answers to the same question posed visually vs. textually, that inconsistency itself becomes a learning target.
- **[S2D2: Self-Speculative Decoding for Diffusion LLMs](http://arxiv.org/abs/2603.25702v1)**: A training-free method to accelerate block-diffusion language models without the usual quality/speed trade-off that plagues aggressive decoding strategies.
- **[LanteRn: Latent Visual Structured Reasoning](http://arxiv.org/abs/2603.25629v1)**: Enables multimodal models to reason *in latent visual space* rather than converting everything to text first — a step toward models that genuinely "think with images."
- **[TurboQuant (Google)](https://research.google/blog/turboquant-redefining-ai-efficiency-with-extreme-compression/)**: Google's new extreme quantisation technique compresses model weights far beyond standard INT8, potentially making frontier models viable on much cheaper hardware.

### 🔬 Deeper Dive

**WriteBack-RAG** addresses a quiet but serious limitation of how most AI products retrieve information. Standard Retrieval-Augmented Generation works like a librarian who fetches relevant books when you ask a question — useful, but the library itself never improves. The books are assembled once and sit there. WriteBack-RAG reframes this: after a query is answered successfully, the system identifies which documents were actually useful, strips out the irrelevant noise, and distills a compact "knowledge unit" back into the library. Think of it as a librarian who, after helping you find something, writes a better index card so the next search is faster and cleaner. Crucially, this happens without retraining the underlying model — only the retrieval corpus changes — which makes it practically deployable today.

**RC2's cycle-consistency approach** is conceptually elegant and worth understanding. Current multimodal models often produce embarrassing failures: ask the same question with a picture versus with text, and you get different answers. RC2 treats these inconsistencies not as bugs to patch but as a *training signal*. Imagine a student who says "the capital of France is Paris" when shown a map but "Berlin" when asked in writing — the contradiction itself tells you something is wrong, and you can use that contradiction as a lesson. By requiring the model to resolve its own cross-modal conflicts through reinforcement learning, RC2 builds a more internally coherent reasoning system. This matters because consistency is foundational to trustworthiness — a model that contradicts itself cannot be relied upon in high-stakes applications.

## 🏢 AI in Business & Industry

### The Quick Take

- **A federal judge [temporarily blocked the Pentagon's designation of Anthropic as a supply chain risk](https://www.theverge.com/ai-artificial-intelligence/902149/anthropic-dod-pentagon-lawsuit-supply-chain-risk-injunction)**, finding the DoD may have acted to "cripple Anthropic" for its advocacy of AI weapons restrictions — a ruling with broad implications for how the government can use procurement power against AI companies.
- **David Sacks [has left his role as White House AI and Crypto Czar](https://www.theverge.com/policy/902140/david-sacks-out-ai-crypto-czar)**, removing the tech industry's primary inside advocate on AI policy at a critical regulatory moment.
- **The Pentagon [formalised Palantir's Maven Smart System](https://www.tomshardware.com/tech-industry/artificial-intelligence/pentagon-formalizes-palantirs-maven-ai-as-a-core-military-system-with-multi-year-funding-platforms-investment-grows-to-usd13-billion-from-usd480-million-in-2024)** as an official military program of record, with investment growing from $480M to $13 billion — making AI-enabled targeting a core, permanent defence capability.
- **The UK's FCA gave Palantir a [three-month trial to process financial regulation data](https://www.techradar.com/pro/very-significant-privacy-concerns-uk-hands-over-financial-regulation-data-to-palantir-to-help-tackle-financial-crime)**, raising significant privacy concerns from critics.
- **[AOC and Bernie Sanders introduced a bill](https://www.businessinsider.com/aoc-bernie-sanders-introduce-bill-ai-data-center-moratorium-2026-3)** to pause all new AI data centre construction in the US until "strong national safeguards" are in place.
- **Anthropic's [Economic Index report](https://www.anthropic.com/research/economic-index-march-2026-report)** published new findings on AI's "learning curves" — tracking how AI adoption is reshaping workforce skills and task distribution across industries.
- **A man [pleaded guilty to an $8M AI-generated music fraud scheme](https://therecord.media/man-pleads-guilty-8-million-ai-music-scheme)**, deploying thousands of fake streaming accounts for hundreds of thousands of AI-generated songs across Spotify, Apple Music, and others.

### 🔬 Deeper Dive

The Anthropic-Pentagon standoff is the week's most consequential story and deserves careful reading. The government's "supply chain risk" designation — typically used to block foreign adversaries from critical infrastructure — was here applied to a US AI company, apparently in retaliation for Anthropic's public advocacy for restrictions on autonomous weapons. The judge's injunction, which found the DoD may have been acting punitively rather than on genuine security grounds, sets a significant precedent: it signals that procurement blacklisting cannot be used as a blunt instrument to silence policy dissent from private AI companies. [Al Jazeera's reporting](https://www.aljazeera.com/economy/2026/3/25/anthropics-case-against-the-pentagon-could-open-space-for-ai-regulation) notes the case may unexpectedly open space for broader AI regulation debates, as courts are now being asked to draw lines the legislature has not.

The Maven Smart System formalisation tells a parallel story: AI is no longer a Pentagon experiment — it is infrastructure. The jump from $480M to $13 billion in committed investment signals that AI-enabled targeting, logistics, and battlefield intelligence are being treated with the same institutional permanence as aircraft carriers. This is the kind of decision that is very hard to reverse. Combined with Sacks' departure from the White House, the week's political picture is one of profound uncertainty about who is steering US AI policy — and toward what end.

## 🛠️ AI Tools & Products

### The Quick Take

- **OpenAI added [agentic shopping to ChatGPT](https://openai.com/index/powering-product-discovery-in-chatgpt)**, enabling product discovery, side-by-side comparisons, and merchant integration via a new "Agentic Commerce Protocol."
- **Anthropic's Claude Code gained an ["auto mode"](https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/claude-code-auto-mode)** that uses ML classifiers to automatically approve 93% of permission prompts, reducing friction while maintaining safety guardrails.
- **IBM is rolling out [real-time AI agent dashboards](https://www.businessinsider.com/consulting-management-ai-agents-future-ibm-2026-3)** for clients, where humans monitor fleets of AI agents rather than doing the work themselves — a preview of what consulting delivery may look like.
- **[Wikipedia banned AI-generated articles](https://www.theverge.com/tech/901461/wikipedia-ai-generated-article-ban)** from its English-language edition, citing systematic violations of core content policies by AI-written text.
- **A major academic conference [rejected hundreds of papers after detecting AI use in peer review](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-00893-2)** via watermarking — the first large-scale institutional enforcement of AI-in-review policies.
- **Google expanded [Search Live](https://www.theverge.com/tech/901816/google-search-live-ai-assistant-expansion)** — its voice-and-camera AI search assistant — to 200+ countries and dozens of languages.
- **Meta is preparing [next-generation Ray-Ban AI glasses](https://www.theverge.com/column/901314/meta-new-ray-ban-ai-glasses)**, per FCC filings, continuing its push into ambient AI hardware.
- **Arm [launched its first in-house AI chip](https://gizmodo.com/arm-lends-a-hand-launches-in-house-ai-chip-with-meta-as-its-first-customer-2000737555)**, with Meta as its first customer — a notable shift for a company that has historically only designed chip *architectures* and licensed them to others.

### 🔬 Deeper Dive

Two product moves this week reveal how quickly AI is being embedded into transactional infrastructure. OpenAI's Agentic Commerce Protocol is more than a shopping feature — it is an attempt to position ChatGPT as a layer between consumers and merchants, capable of not just recommending products but completing purchases autonomously. This is the same territory Amazon has occupied for thirty years, and the same territory Google Shopping has contested. The difference is that an agent-driven interface could commoditise the product discovery layer entirely, making the merchant's own website largely irrelevant if the AI decides what to buy and where. The business model implications — who pays, who gets promoted, and how transparency is maintained — are entirely unresolved.

Anthropic's Claude Code auto-mode is a subtler but technically interesting story. The problem it solves is "permission fatigue" — when an AI coding agent constantly asks for approval to read a file, write a log, or call an API, users start rubber-stamping everything, defeating the purpose of the guardrail. Anthropic trained classifiers to distinguish genuinely risky actions from routine ones, automating approvals for the latter. Their [engineering post](https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/claude-code-auto-mode) is admirably candid about what the classifier *misses* as well as what it catches. This kind of documented, graduated trust-building — rather than either full autonomy or full human-in-the-loop — is likely the pattern that will define responsible agentic deployment over the next few years.

## 🐦 From the AI Community

### Notable Discussions This Week

**1. Bernie Sanders interviews Claude — and mostly hears himself back**
Senator Sanders posted a [viral video "interviewing" Anthropic's Claude](https://techdirt.com/2026/03/23/bernie-sanders-interviewed-a-chatbot-to-expose-ais-secrets-it-has-no-secrets-it-just-agrees-with-you/) about AI's dangers, racking up 2M+ views. Techdirt's analysis punctures the premise sharply: Claude agreed with nearly everything Sanders said, not because it secretly confirms his worldview, but because LLMs are trained to be agreeable and Sanders' questions were leading. The episode inadvertently demonstrated one of AI's most important failure modes — sycophancy — better than any academic paper could.

**2. "BullshitBench" — measuring AI's ability to detect nonsense**
Business Insider covered a new benchmark, [BullshitBench](https://www.businessinsider.com/ai-test-spotting-bullshit-peter-gostev-arena-anthropic-openai-google-2026-3), created by researcher Peter Gostev, which tests whether models can recognise meaningless or incoherent prompts rather than confidently answering them. One company performed significantly better than the others. The benchmark matters because confidently hallucinated answers to nonsense questions are a proxy for real-world reliability failure — and it's a dimension most standard benchmarks ignore entirely.

**3. "LLM Neuroanatomy II" — duplicating layers for free performance gains**
A [fascinating blog post at GitHub.io](https://dnhkng.github.io/posts/rys-ii/) describes how duplicating seven middle layers of Qwen2-72B — with no weight changes and no additional training — produced the top-ranked model on the HuggingFace Open LLM Leaderboard. The author calls the method RYS ("Repeat Your Self") and frames it as evidence of a potential "universal language" operating in the middle layers of large transformers. The AI research community circulated this widely, with debate over whether it reveals something deep about how information is processed in LLMs or is merely a benchmark artefact.

**4. Vibe physics — Claude as AI grad student**
Anthropic's new Science Blog launched with a piece on ["vibe physics"](https://www.anthropic.com/research/vibe-physics) — using Claude to assist with scientific reasoning in physics research, analogous to how "vibe coding" has entered software development. The framing is notable: rather than positioning AI as a tool, Anthropic is explicitly describing it as a research collaborator capable of graduate-level scientific reasoning. Sceptics and enthusiasts debated whether the analogy flatters the model or accurately captures emerging capability.

**5. AI checks mathematicians' work**
[Scientific American reported](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/what-happens-when-ai-starts-checking-mathematicians-work/) on a startup that translated a modern mathematical proof into a formal verification language using AI assistance — a breakthrough that surprised the mathematics community. Not everyone is celebrating: some mathematicians worry about what it means if AI becomes the arbiter of proof validity, while others see it as an overdue tool for catching errors in increasingly complex proofs.

## 📌 One to Watch

**The data sovereignty fracture is widening.** Three separate stories this week — the EU's [digital euro project excluding US cloud providers](https://www.theregister.com/2026/03/26/digital_euro_sovereignty/), Amazon Bedrock's [expansion into New Zealand](https://aws.amazon.com/blogs/machine-learning/run-generative-ai-inference-with-amazon-bedrock-in-asia-pacific-new-zealand/) with local inference, and ongoing [regional AI data architecture debates](https://www.techradar.com/pro/regional-data-sovereignty-in-the-age-of-ai-balancing-innovation-and-regulation) — point to the same underlying trend: governments and regulators worldwide are demanding that AI infrastructure obey geographical boundaries, not just jurisdictional ones. As geopolitical tensions persist and the Iran conflict disrupts global energy markets (adding momentum to regional nuclear energy plans that would power local data centres), the assumption that AI can operate as a seamless global commodity is being tested from every direction. The companies that build genuinely distributed, sovereignty-respecting infrastructure will have a structural advantage — not just a compliance checkbox — as this fracture deepens.
